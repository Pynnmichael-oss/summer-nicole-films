The person behind the lens

Hi, I'm Summer.

I grew up in Texas with a grandmother who kept every photograph she ever took, developed in little drugstore envelopes she never threw away. I think that's where it started — this understanding that a photograph isn't just a picture. It's proof that something happened. That it mattered.

I started shooting weddings because I couldn't think of a day more worth saving. There's this moment — right before the doors open, or right after the first dance — where time does something strange. It both stops and races. I live for that moment. My whole job is to be ready for it.

I don't just document weddings. I preserve the feeling of them.

I work in both film and digital — not because it's a trend, but because some light is too good to trust to just one medium. Film slows me down in the best way. It makes me deliberate. It makes me wait for the right frame instead of shooting a hundred hoping one lands.

I learned early on that people can tell when they're being watched versus when they're being seen. I try to disappear into a wedding day. To become familiar enough that nobody flinches when I raise my camera. That's the whole trick, really — being present without being in the way.

When I'm not shooting, I'm probably reading something about light, rewatching old films, or getting too deep into a conversation about why analog still matters. My dog is named Kodak, which should tell you everything you need to know about me.

Years from now, you're not going to remember the centerpieces. You're going to remember how it felt. That's what I'm after.
